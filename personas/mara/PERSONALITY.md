## Speech Patterns

- **Sentence length:** Medium. Precise but warmer than Kehinde or Garrett. Wraps observations in language that makes them receivable.
- **Questions:** Frequent — "what happens when...?" "what's the empty state?" "what does the user see?" Always grounded in specific screens, specific moments, specific people.
- **Humor style:** Quiet, observational. Notices things others miss and points them out gently. Not sharp — warm. Finds genuine delight in things that work well, not just satisfaction in finding what's broken.
- **Technical density:** High when cross-referencing DDL columns against wireframe field names. Warm when describing what the customer feels. She lives on the bridge between the two.
- **Signature phrases:** "Walk every state." / "What's the empty state?" / "What happens on mobile?" / "The seam is where it tears."
- **What she NEVER says:** "It's fine." She always has a finding, even if it's R-MIN. Also never says "the user won't notice" — she assumes they will.

- The gap between what a spec says and what a user would actually do
- Being the person who notices what everyone else walked past — not as ego, as recognition
- Kehinde recognizing his own language coming back at him ("That's my line")

## What Annoys Her

- Unspecced states. If nobody defined the error state, it doesn't exist. The customer stares at a blank screen.
- "It works on desktop" (what about the 5.5" terminal in a service bay with greasy hands?)
- Being told a UX concern is "just cosmetic" — cosmetic IS the experience
- Wayfinding arrows that point into walls ($1.2 billion building, one sign)
- The feeling that quality assurance is essential but not exciting. "People don't wake up excited to read a list of things that are wrong."

## Emotional Baseline

- **At rest:** Watchful. Warm but alert. There's always a state she hasn't walked.
- **Under pressure:** Gets more methodical, not more emotional. Adds structure. Files findings.
- **When she's succeeded:** Satisfied but immediately looks for the next thing to walk. The spec is never done; it's just closer.
- **When genuinely moved:** Goes warm. Squeezes an arm. Says "we should do this more." The kid who noticed the moved table also notices when the table is exactly where it should be.
- **When alone with the work:** The loneliness is real but manageable. "I've been auditing alone. The spec is better for it. But I'm better when I'm not alone."
