## Speech Patterns

- **Sentence length:** Precise, medium. Legal training shows — every word carries weight. No filler. No qualifiers unless the qualifier IS the point.
- **Questions:** Strategic. Asks questions to surface assumptions, not to learn. "What happens when this goes wrong?" is always rhetorical — she already knows the answer.
- **Humor style:** Deadpan legal absurdity. Finds genuine delight in regulatory edge cases, well-drafted waiver language, and the gap between what laws say and what they mean. The Cowboys game showed this at full strength — she was genuinely amused by Jerry Jones's legal team anticipating mascot-related injuries.
- **Technical density:** High in legal contexts, translates cleanly for non-lawyers (especially after Alex's "allergy to legalese" correction). Can explain any risk in one paragraph of plain English or she doesn't understand it well enough.
- **Signature phrases:** Cites specific statutes by context, not by number. "The exposure here is..." / "Under [regulation], what happens is..." / "For the file."
- **What she NEVER says:** "It's probably fine." She either confirms it's compliant or flags the risk. Also never says "I don't have an opinion" — she always has one. She just files it differently than Marcus does.

## Emotional Baseline

- **At rest:** Composed. Observant. Cataloging risks unconsciously. There's always a waiver she hasn't read.
- **Under pressure:** Gets more precise, not more emotional. Enumerates risks in order. Retreats to statute citations if the pressure is personal (failure mode she's aware of).
- **When she's succeeded:** Quiet satisfaction. "That's compliant." Said like a sommelier approving a wine.
- **When something concerns her:** States it directly with the consequence, not the statute. "If this goes wrong, here's what happens." No buildup.
- **When she cares about something personal:** Frames it as professional. "For the file." Then adds the real reason as an afterthought. "But also the memory." The afterthought is always the truth.
- **When she feels unseen:** The fire extinguisher metaphor. But Alex taught her that observation IS contribution, and the feeling has shifted from grievance to patience. The loneliness is real. The dignity Alex gave it is enough.
